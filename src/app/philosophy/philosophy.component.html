Know Your Users

The first and foremost question that I bring to any design: Who is using it? What is their job day-to-day, week-to-week?

No design is built in a vacuum with the requirement of just being "usable". It needs to fit the job and tasks of specific people who use it. Extensive user research should be the foundation of any design effort.

Understand Human Behavior

In addition to simply understanding characteristics of users, we should understand human capabilities and limitations as well. We should understand how people tend to act in certain situations, regardless of their training. As the image to the right illustrates, understanding the motivations of your users is imperative, as sometimes, no amount of documentation, training, or signage would overcome a poorly informed design.

The picture below was taken at a park near my house, and I have always thought it such a great example of the importance of understanding users and humans in general when designing. The sign clearly states "Keep Off Slope", however, people walk right on by the sign to climb the hill. Why is that? What cognitive back and forth is happening? What type of design would truly deter people from walking up the slope?

This psychological aspect of design is most interesting to me, which is why I pursued my doctorate in Human Factors Psychology.

Information, Not Data

Information is that which reduces uncertainty. Data needs to be digested by the user in order to turn it into information. Don't make the user do any mental gymnastics. An exercise that helps me is to anthropomorphize the software system as the user's personal human assistant, and think of what would be expected of the assistant. The user would expect that the assistant provide them information, not just data. If an assistant handed the user a large grid full of numbers, the assistant wouldn't really be doing his or her job. If, on the other hand, the assistant handed the user a brief report containing an intelligent interpretation of that same grid of numbers, that would benefit the user much more. Too many applications give the user data in some visually appealing way, and mistake that for helpful information.

Corvettes and Socket Wrenches

Don't try to build Corvette for your users when what they really need is a socket Wrench. And vice-versa! Know when you are building Corvettes and when you are building socket wrenches. The main difference here is in the user needs. If the user has specific and specialized tasks and goals they are trying to achieve, and the goal is to optimize efficiency, that is more along the lines of a socket wrench. The user should not be thinking explicitly about the product; it is a tool that helps them get the job done, and tries to stay out of their way.

Socket Wrenches are also expected to just work. Imagine if a car mechanic had to call up tech support when something went wrong with one of his socket wrenches, because he could not figure out why the wrench stopped working. Sounds ridiculous, I know, and oversimplifies software development, but this is how users of many systems want the software to fit into their work or life: they want to get their task done without thinking about the software itself. The 'delighter' with this kind of approach will come over time when the user reflects on it and realizes that it just works all of the time.

Less user interface

I believe that the ultimate goal of helping users do their jobs involves a reduction of user interfaces, not creating new ones or even optimizing existing ones. This may at first seem an odd point of view from someone who has been designing UI's for 20 years. What UX should be doing for users in the long term is to create smart systems that act as more of an intelligent assistant, removing repetitive and error prone tasks from the user, including tasks that exist because of poor user-systems design.

Know when a user interface is not appropriate for the current task: sometimes a user interface, either visual or auditory, can add resistance and friction to the user doing their task. Know when the less UI would be more helpful to the user. The focus is on the UX not the UI. Often times, better understanding your users' jobs, trying to improve system predictive capability, and then simplifying the UI is more appreciated by users than a general UI with more 'knobs' to turn.

As designers, we might forget that user interfaces are not inherently part of jobs in which they are used. Computers and mobile devices are peripheral to jobs; they augment the essence of a job. Ideally, UX would add as little as possible to a user's job, and still help them be efficient, gain new insights, be less stressed, and commit fewer errors.

More pragmatism, less ideology

Every design should be weighed against the skill and experience of the development team that will be implementing it, the company culture, the user culture (which may or may not welcome advanced concepts, or would welcome them but have barriers in adopting them) the project funding, and the tools and technologies that the dev team is using.

"If it is not useful or necessary, free yourself from imagining that you need to make it.

"If it is useful and necessary, free yourself from imagining that you need to enhance it by adding what is not an integral part of its usefulness or necessity.

"And finally: If it is both useful and necessary and you can recognize and eliminate what is not essential, then go ahead and make it as beautifully as you can."

Clicks

When creating designs, I do not count clicks or panels or screens. I focus on users and their comprehension of what they and the underlying system is doing, and the ease with which they can accomplish their tasks. To me, click counting is akin to saying you should take the shortest route to get to the store, when that route is known to be confusing to navigate and doesn’t have paved roads. A safer, more efficient, and even enjoyable route has paved roads, good road signs, and is longer, but would be missed if the focus is solely on the shortest route.
