Know Your Users

The first and foremost question that I bring to any design: Who is using it? What is their job day-to-day, week-to-week?

No design is built in a vacuum with the requirement of just being "usable". It needs to fit the job and tasks of specific people who use it. Extensive user research should be the foundation of any design effort.

Understand Human Behavior

As the image to the right illustrates, understanding the motivations of your users is imperative, as sometimes, no amount of documentation, training, or signage would overcome a poorly informed design. We should seek to understand human capabilities and limitations; understand how people tend to act in certain situations, regardless of their training.

The picture below was taken at a park near my house, and I have always thought it such a great example of the importance of understanding users and human cognition in general when designing. The sign clearly states "Keep Off Slope", however, people walk right on by the sign to climb the hill. Why is that? What is the cognitive cost-benefit analysis that the person is unconsciously doing? What type of approach, beyond signage, would truly deter people from walking up the slope?

This psychological aspect of design is most interesting to me, which is why I pursued my doctorate in Human Factors Psychology.

Information, Not Data

Information is that which reduces uncertainty. Data needs to be digested by the user in order to turn it into information. And so, the more information we can give users, the less cognitive burden we place on them.

And knowing what constitutes information requires a thorough understanding of users.

Corvettes and Socket Wrenches

I use these two as metaphors for software intended for pleasure and entertainment vs. software that should be more of a transparent tool.

Don't try to build "Corvette" for your users when what they really need is a "socket wrench". And vice-versa! Know when you are building Corvettes and when you are building socket wrenches. The main difference here is in the user needs. If the user has specific and specialized needs, and the goal is to optimize efficiency, that is more along the lines of a socket wrench. In other words, the user should not be thinking explicitly about the product; it is a tool that helps them get the job done, and tries to stay out of their way.

"Perfection is attained not when there is no longer anything to add, but when there is no longer anything to take away." Antoine De Saint Exupery

Less user interface

I believe that the ultimate goal of helping users do their jobs involves a reduction of user interfaces, not creating new ones or even optimizing existing ones. The long-term goal of UX should be to create smart systems that act more like intelligent assistants, removing repetitive and error prone tasks from the user.

Know when a user interface is not appropriate for the current task: sometimes a user interface, either visual or auditory, can add cognitive friction. Know when less UI would be more helpful to the user. The focus is on the UX not the UI.

As designers, we might forget that user interfaces are not inherently part of jobs in which they are used. Computers and mobile devices are peripheral to jobs; they augment the essence of a job. Ideally, UX would add as little as possible to a user's job, while helping them be efficient, gain new insights, be less stressed, and commit fewer errors.

Be Pragmatic

Every design should be weighed against the skill and experience of the development team that will be implementing it, the company culture, the user culture (which may or may not welcome changes/improvements, or would welcome them but be constrained in how they can adopt changes) the project funding, and the technologies that the development team is using.

"If it is not useful or necessary, free yourself from imagining that you need to make it.

"If it is useful and necessary, free yourself from imagining that you need to enhance it by adding what is not an integral part of its usefulness or necessity.

"And finally: If it is both useful and necessary and you can recognize and eliminate what is not essential, then go ahead and make it as beautifully as you can."

Rule of thumb for Shaker creations, from Shaker Built by Paul Rochleau & June Sprigg

Clicks

When creating designs, I tend not to use the number of clicks, panels, or screens as usability metrics. Instead, I focus on users' comprehension of the task flow, and the ease with which they can accomplish their goals. To me, click counting is akin to saying you should take the shortest route to get to the store, when that route is known to be confusing to navigate and doesnâ€™t have paved roads. A safer, more efficient, and even enjoyable route has paved roads, good road signs, and is longer, but would be missed if the focus is solely on the shortest route.
